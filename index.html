A novel device for quantifying tremor during focused ultrasound thalamotomy (2024 - present)

PI: Daniel Cleary, MD, PhD

The objective of this work is to find a way to objectively quantify tremor in patients presenting for MRI-guided focused utrasound thalamotomies (MRgFUS). This is a procedure in wich an area of the brain is burned with high-intensity sound waves, and patients obtain immediate releif from their tremor. The target in the brain, however is not direclty visible on imaging, and though landmarks to navigate to the area have improved, the procedure relies heavily on lower-energy "tests" which temporarily injures neurons in the area the surgeon belives their target to be. Tremor control is evaluated for improvement. If there is improvement detected, the surgeon will expand that lesion and administer higher energy to make it permanent.

Current clinical tremor evaluation relies on subjective assessment using a paper-drawn spiral, as well as simply observing the tremor while doing a task such as holding a cup close to the patients mouth (simulating drinking). This acceleromter-based device provide objective, quantifiable metrics to elucidate specific amplitude of improvmeent after each test dose to ensure improvment. This device is able to infer smaller improvments more sensitively than the human eye can detect, which may help the surgeon adjust their target to gain the largest decrease in tremor with the smallest extent of any side effects.

Additionaly, we are exploring the potential for this device to be used in the neurology clinic to classify and potentialy diagnose different types of tremor/movements using typical "signautures" or patterns an accelerometer can pick up typical for that movement. This device can also be used to track the progression and severity of tremor over time, and to detail the effect medications has on the tremor.

The figure above shows the improvement of tremor between focued-ultrasound ablations. The first line of drawings are spirals, outlining the tremors which progressively improve with treatment. The graphs under the spiral drawings show the magnitude of the tremor as detected by the device, which progressivley improve. This is shown by the peaks (outlined in yellow) greatly reducing to almost nothing. The graph on the right shows the percent reduction in tremor after each treatment dose.

(BPhil Thesis) Differences in excitatory and inhibitory neuron oxygen metabolism (2020 - present)

PI: Alberto Vazquez, PhD

The objective of this work is to elucidate the differences in metabolic demands of the two major neuronal types in the brain: excitatory neurons and inhibitory neurons. Many neuronal diseases such as Epilepsy, Alzheimer’s Disease, and Schizophrenia are thought to exhibit dysregulation between excitatory and inhibitory neurons; investigating differences in metabolism between the two types of neurons can lead to discovery of targeted therapies for the aforementioned diseases.

This objective was investigated using wide field optical imaging, laser doppler flowmetry, and optogenetics (to selectively activate neuronal subpopulations). Optogenetics includes genetically inserting a light-sensitive ion channel in the membranes of a target neuronal population. Thus, when stimulated with a certain wavelength of light, the neurons within the neuronal population of interest depolarize.

I completed this project as my thesis for the BPhil degree program, through the University of Pittsburgh Honors College (more information here). Upon completion of this project in April 2022, I wrote a thesis and defended my work in front of a panel of experts in the field as well as a visiting professor from an outside university.

In vivo assessment of cortical neural and metabolic activity after pediatric cardiac arrest (2018 - present)

PI: Alberto Vazquez, PhD

The objective of this project is to assess the function of the neurovascular unit after cardiac arrest. Neuronal local field potentials were measured in vivo using microelectrodes, cortical blood flow was measured using laser doppler flowmetry, and local metabolism was measured using optical imaging. We finished recording and analyzing the data, and are currently preparing it for publication.

My role on this project is data analysis and manuscript preparation. I developed the MATLAB scripts used to analyze Flavoprotein Autofluorescence Images (FAI) and Local Field potential (LFP) signals from all the animal subjects, analyzed the data, and generated the figures. I am currently working on the manuscript with my mentor.

I presented this work at two conferences and received the “Best Undergraduate Research” award from the University of Pittsburgh Rehabilitation Institute.

Resuscitation with Epinephrine Worsens Microvascular Constriction after Experimental Pediatric Cardiac Arrest (2018 - present)

GitHub: Code Here
PIs: Dr. Onome Ohgifobibi, MD and Dr. Mioara Manole, MD

This project assesses the microvascular and long-term neurological effect of epinephrine in a rodent model of cardiac arrest.

Epinephrine, the medication which increases the rate of resuscitation in victims of cardiac arrest, has been recently identified as a potential contributor to brain injury. The detrimental effects of epinephrine have been postulated to result from cerebral microvascular constriction.

For this study, we randomized the rats to receive epinephrine vs. normal saline at resuscitation from cardiac arrest. We quantified the microvascular diameters and brain oxygenation to predict neurological outcomes. Our results suggest that administration of epinephrine at resuscitation increases arteriolar constriction, stalls blood flow in capillaries, and worsens functional outcome after cardiac arrest.

I assisted in capturing in vivo multi-photon microscopy images of the cortex, developed a python program to measure the diameter of cortical capillaries, and quantified the capillary diameters at baseline and after cardiac arrest.

Adenosine Administration with a Stopcock Technique Delivers Lower-Than-Intended Drug Doses (2018)

Publication: Annals of Emergency Medicine
PIs: Dr. Nate Weberding, DO and Dr. Mioara Manole, MD

The objective of this project was to assess if administration of adenosine through a stopcock device delivers a lower dose of adenosine than intended, due to some of the drug remaining in the stopcock.

Adenosine is a medication administered to stop supraventricular tachycardia. This medication needs to be rapidly administered intravenously to reach the supraventricular node of the heart. In small children, the volume of adenosine to be administered is quite small, and thus may not reach the patient due to the stopcock not administering a small part of the medication.

I assisted Dr. Weberding in experiments carried out in a pharmacology laboratory, where I measured the concentration of adenosine in samples delivered through the stopcock device. We used Nuclear Magnetic Resonance Spectroscopy to quantify the amount of adenosine in the samples. Click on the link above for the publication; My work was acknowledged in the manuscript.

Embedded Systems Engineering Intern, Medtronic (2020)

Product: CyoConsole

From June to August 2020, I worked in a team of approximately 100 engineers to develop a cryoablation device. Cardiac ablation is the process of interrupting dysfunctional electrical circuits in the heart for the treatment of conditions such as atrial fibrillation. Scar tissue from these lesions have a higher resistance than the surrounding cardiac muscle tissue, so if there is an area of the heart that conducts current when it should not, a lesion can be created using an ablation device.

In this device, the user interacts with the main computer screen to input what they want the device to do (ablate, advance the catheter, etc.) which then gets transmitted to a microcontroller via a wireless communication protocol. Upon receiving the command from the main computer, the microcontroller carries out the functions of the device. The communication system is vital for the ablation device because it sends the surgeon's control commands from the computer to the catheter. I worked on the communication between the microcontroller and the computer by packaging, documenting architecture of, and testing the code.

Accelerometer-based Tremor Detection (June 2024 - present)

GitHub: Code Here
Paper: Pre-publication Here

As part of my research with OHSU, I developed a device which detects tremor to improve the diagnosis and surgical treatment of tremor (see projects tab for more information).

It consists of two parts, a pen with an accelerometer (a device to detect movements) attached to it, as well as a tablet where the patient draws sprials and lines. The tremor is analyzed in two modalities: the first is by obtaining a power spectral density (PSD) from the accelerometer, and the second is by analyzing the shape of the spiral on the screen, and obtaining a PSD in the spatial sense. Tremor qualities and magnitude are extracted from those features. See the Github link for code and additional information.

Portable EKG Device (Jan 2022 - May 2022)

As part of my senior design capstone course, I designed an EKG amplifier and filter with a group of 4 classmates. The device obtained the cardiac signal through leads on the skin, amplified it, and sent it over Bluetooth to an iPhone application for viewing by the patient and care providers. A working prototype was built by the end of the semester.

The design consisted of 4 stages: the first was an instrumentation amplifier with a gain of 40V/V. The second and third stages were 2nd order butterworth active low pass filter (LPF) and high pass filter (HPF), to create an effective band pass filtering effect. The final stage was an op-amp amplifier circuit with 200V/V gain. The input to the circuit was the percutaneous electrical signals obtained from 3M ECG stickers placed on the right and left arms.

The output from the hardware bioamplification unit was then sent over Bluetooth from a microcontroller (ATMEGA328P) to an iPhone application. The iPhone application recieved the waveform and displayed it on the phone, along with the heartrate computed from the waveform. Accident Reporting Application (2020)

GitHub: Code Here

During my years in the National Ski Patrol, I identified a need to develop an application that allows the skiers to alert the ski patrol staff in a timely manner when there is an injury on the slopes. Traditionally, the skiers first alert a lift attendant who calls the Ski Patrol center. A message is then sent to the ski patrollers on the slopes, which can cause delays in responding to accidents.

I created an innovative phone application with a small group of people consisting of a simple form which the user fills out. The form is then transmitted immediately to the resort ski patrol headquarter and to the ski patrollers on duty. The proposed application can decrease ski patrol response time and improve patient outcome in serious injuries. We are currently in the process of reviewing this application with the Ski Patrol leadership.
